Reading Notes on Kingman (1982) — The Coalescent

Theorem 1 and the first four pages (pp. 235–238)

J. F. C. Kingman (1982), “The Coalescent,” Stochastic Processes and their Applications 13:235–248.
Focus: Theorem 1 and pp. 235–238.

1 0. Overview

  • Object. The \(n\)-coalescent is a continuous-time Markov chain (CTMC) on partitions of \(\{1,\dots,n\}\). From a partition with \(k\) blocks, one merge occurs at total rate \(d_k=\binom{k}{2}\).
  • Theorem 1 (core). The process decomposes into two independent parts:
    1. a pure death process \(D_t\) on \(\{1,\dots,n\}\) with death rate \(d_k=\binom{k}{2}\) from \(k\to k-1\);
    2. a jump chain \((\mathcal S_k)_{k=n,\dots,1}\) that, at level \(k\), picks uniformly among the \(\binom{k}{2}\) possible merges.
      The state at time \(t\) is [ R_t=S_{D_t}. ] For a level-\(k\) partition \(\xi\) with block sizes \((A_1,\dots,A_k)\), [ (S_k=)=_{i=1}^k A_i!. ]
  • Immediate payoff. Probability at time \(t\) factorizes: [ (R_t=)=(D_t=k)(S_k=). ]

2 1. Key definition

2.1 1.1 Continuous-time Markov chain (CTMC)

A process \(\{X_t\}\) on state space \(S\) with generator \(Q=(q_{ij})\): - holding time in \(i\) is \(\mathrm{Exp}(-q_{ii})\); - upon leaving \(i\), next state is \(j\) with prob. \(q_{ij}/(-q_{ii})\).

In the \(n\)-coalescent, if there are \(k\) blocks, the holding time is \(\mathrm{Exp}(\binom{k}{2})\).

2.2 1.2 Jump chain

The discrete-time chain of post-jump states: [ {Y_{m+1}=jY_m=i}=. ] For the coalescent at level \(k\), each pairwise merge has probability \(1/\binom{k}{2}\).

3 2. Theorem 1

  • Statement. \(R_t=\mathcal S_{D_t}\), with \(D_t\) (pure death) and \((\mathcal S_k)\) (uniform merge jump chain) independent.
  • Consequences.
    • Absorption time (MRCA time): [ T=_{k=2}^n _k,_k!()  ]
    • Snapshot at time \(t\): [ (R_t=)=(D_t=k)(S_k=). ]
    • Computational simplicity. Time (exponentials) and structure (uniform merge over partitions) are decoupled.

4 3. Micro-example (\(n=4\))

  • Level \(4\to3\): \(\binom{4}{2}=6\) possible merges, each prob. \(1/6\).
  • Level \(3\to2\): \(\binom{3}{2}=3\) possible merges, each prob. \(1/3\).

At \(k=2\), two shapes: - \((3,1)\): total probability \(2/3\) (four labeled partitions, each \(1/6\)). - \((2,2)\): total probability \(1/3\) (three labeled partitions, each \(1/9\)).

This matches counting under uniform merges and the formula for \(\Pr(\mathcal S_2=\xi)\).

5 4. Quick answers to the prompt

5.1 4.1 What is a CTMC? What is its jump chain?

  • CTMC: continuous-time Markov process with exponential holding times governed by generator \(Q\); next state on a jump is chosen with probability proportional to the outgoing rates.
  • Jump chain: the embedded discrete-time chain recording the sequence of states after each jump. In the coalescent, at level \(k\), it is uniform over pairs.

5.2 4.2 Why is this paper a major contribution?

  1. Clean mathematical model of genealogies. Genealogical “coalescing backward in time” becomes a transparent CTMC on partitions with explicit rates \(d_k=\binom{k}{2}\).
  2. Theorem 1’s factorization. Separates time (pure death) from structure (uniform merges), turning many problems into elementary computations.
  3. Consistency and extensibility. The framework scales across sample sizes and connects naturally to exchangeable random partitions (via paintbox), fueling a broad theory and many applications.
